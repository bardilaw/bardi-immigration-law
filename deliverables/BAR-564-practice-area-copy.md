### A green card, explained simply

A green card gives you lawful permanent residency in the United States. It means you can live and work here permanently, travel more freely, and — after a few years — apply to become a U.S. citizen. For most families, it is the goal that everything else builds toward.

Getting one is rarely simple. There are several different paths to a green card, and the right path depends on your family, your work, your history, and your immigration status today. Choosing the wrong path, or filing before you are eligible, can cost you years. As a boutique green card attorney serving Georgia, Alabama, and the wider Southeast, Attorney Bardi helps you find the correct path the first time and build the strongest possible case for it.

### The main paths to a green card

There is no single application that fits everyone. Most green cards come through one of these paths:

- **Family-based** — A U.S. citizen or green card holder petitions for a spouse, child, parent, or sibling.
- **Employment-based** — An employer sponsors you, or you qualify based on your skills or investment.
- **Asylum** — People granted asylum can apply for a green card, usually one year after approval.
- **VAWA** — Survivors of abuse by a U.S. citizen or permanent resident spouse, parent, or child can self-petition, confidentially and without the abuser's knowledge.
- **Diversity visa** — A yearly lottery for people from countries with low rates of immigration to the U.S.

Each path has its own forms, deadlines, and eligibility rules. Some allow you to apply from inside the United States (adjustment of status); others require processing at a consulate abroad.

### What the process looks like

Most cases follow a similar shape: confirm you are eligible, file the petition that establishes your category, wait for a visa to become available, then file the green card application itself. Timelines vary widely — from under a year to several years — depending on your category and country of origin. Prior immigration violations, unlawful presence, or a past removal order do not automatically end your case, but they do require careful strategy and sometimes a waiver. Attorney Bardi identifies these issues early, so nothing derails your case later.

### What asylum is

Asylum is protection for people who cannot safely return to their home country. If you have been harmed — or fear you will be harmed — because of your race, religion, nationality, political opinion, or membership in a particular social group, you may qualify for asylum in the United States. If your case is approved, you can stay in the country lawfully, work, and later apply for a green card.

Asylum is one of the most fact-sensitive areas of immigration law. Two people from the same country can have very different cases. As an asylum lawyer serving Alabama, Georgia, and the Southeast, Attorney Bardi helps you understand whether your experience fits the legal definition and how to prove it.

### Who qualifies

To win asylum, you generally must show three things: that you have suffered past harm or genuinely fear future harm, that the harm is tied to one of the five protected reasons above, and that your own government cannot or will not protect you. The legal standard is detailed, and the evidence matters as much as the story. Country-conditions reports, medical records, witness statements, and your own credible testimony all play a role.

### Affirmative vs. defensive asylum

There are two ways an asylum case moves forward:

- **Affirmative asylum** — You apply on your own with USCIS, before the government has placed you in removal proceedings. You attend an interview with an asylum officer.
- **Defensive asylum** — You request asylum as a defense after removal (deportation) proceedings have already begun. Your case is heard by an immigration judge in court.

The path you are on changes the strategy, the evidence, and the deadlines. Attorney Bardi handles both.

### The one-year filing deadline

This is the rule that surprises people most: in general, you must apply for asylum within **one year of your last arrival** in the United States. Miss it, and you may lose the chance — unless a narrow exception applies, such as changed circumstances in your country or extraordinary personal circumstances. If you are near or past the one-year mark, do not wait to get advice. Acting early protects your options.

### What a removal proceeding is

A removal proceeding is the formal court process the government uses to try to deport someone from the United States. It begins when the Department of Homeland Security files a document called a **Notice to Appear (NTA)** in immigration court. The NTA lists the reasons the government believes you should be removed and tells you to appear before an immigration judge.

These cases are serious, and the consequences of losing are long-lasting. But an NTA is not the end. It is the beginning of a process where you have the right to defend yourself — and where strong, early legal representation makes a real difference. As a removal defense attorney serving Georgia, Alabama, and the Southeast, Attorney Bardi represents clients in both detained and non-detained cases, from the first hearing through trial and appeal.

### What to do if you receive a Notice to Appear

Read it carefully and note the hearing date — missing a hearing can result in a removal order issued against you in your absence. Do not sign anything you do not understand. Do not assume you have no options. The single most important step is to talk to an immigration attorney quickly, because the strongest defenses depend on evidence gathered early. If you or a family member is **detained**, time is even more urgent; contact us right away so we can move to seek release.

### Common defenses against removal

Many people in removal proceedings are eligible for relief that lets them stay. The most common defenses include:

- **Asylum** and related protection for people who fear harm in their home country.
- **Cancellation of removal** — for certain long-term residents who meet strict presence and hardship requirements.
- **Adjustment of status** — becoming a permanent resident, often through a family relationship, even while in proceedings.
- **Bond hearings** — arguing for release from detention while your case continues.
- **Waivers and appeals**, including review by the Board of Immigration Appeals.

Which defense fits depends entirely on the facts of your life. Attorney Bardi reviews every available form of relief — not just the obvious one.
