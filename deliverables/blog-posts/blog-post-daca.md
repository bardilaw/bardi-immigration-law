# DACA in 2026: What Georgia Dreamers Need to Know

As of May 2026, DACA renewals are still being processed by USCIS — but the program remains in legal uncertainty that has persisted since 2021. If you're a Dreamer in Georgia, the most important thing you can do right now is stay informed, renew on time, and work with a qualified DACA attorney in Georgia who can help you navigate what comes next.

Georgia is home to approximately 19,000 active DACA recipients. This guide explains what DACA currently protects, who qualifies to renew, where the litigation stands, and what steps to take today.

---

## What DACA Actually Protects

DACA — Deferred Action for Childhood Arrivals — is not a visa, a green card, or a path to citizenship. Understanding exactly what it provides is essential to protecting your rights.

**What DACA currently provides:**
- **Work authorization.** DACA grants Employment Authorization Documents (EADs), allowing you to work legally in the United States.
- **Deportation deferral.** DACA defers removal action for the period covered by your approval. It is not a legal status — it is an exercise of prosecutorial discretion by the federal government.
- **Driver's licenses and state IDs.** Most states, including Georgia, issue driver's licenses to valid DACA recipients.

**What DACA does not provide:**
- A path to lawful permanent residence or citizenship on its own.
- Protection if you leave the U.S. without approved advance parole.
- Any guarantee of renewal — USCIS can deny applications for criminal history, national security concerns, or changed policy guidance.

If you currently hold DACA, that protection is real and enforceable for the duration of your valid approval. But it requires active maintenance.

## Who Qualifies for DACA Renewal in 2026

As of May 2026, USCIS is processing **renewal applications only** — no new initial applications are being accepted due to ongoing federal court orders. To qualify for renewal, you generally must meet the following criteria:

- **Age:** You were under 31 years old on June 15, 2012, and were at least 15 when you first applied.
- **Continuous residence:** You have continuously resided in the United States since June 15, 2007.
- **Physical presence:** You were physically present in the U.S. on June 15, 2012, and at the time you file for renewal.
- **Education or military service:** You are currently enrolled in school, hold a high school diploma or GED, or received an honorable discharge from the U.S. armed forces.
- **Criminal history:** No felony convictions, no significant misdemeanors, and no more than two minor misdemeanors.
- **Prior DACA approval:** You have an existing approval on record with USCIS.

USCIS recommends filing **150 to 180 days before your EAD expires.** Processing times fluctuate, and a lapse means loss of work authorization — don't wait until the last minute.

## The Current Legal Status of DACA

DACA's future remains legally uncertain as of May 2026 — and has been since a federal district court in Texas ruled the program unlawful in 2021.

Here's where things stand: In October 2022, the Fifth Circuit Court of Appeals upheld the lower court's finding that the original 2012 DACA policy was unlawful under the Administrative Procedure Act, while sending the matter back to the district court to evaluate a revised rule finalized in late 2022. That litigation remains active. The revised rule has been enjoined for new applicants, though renewals for existing recipients have continued under separate court orders.

The practical result: USCIS continues processing **renewals** for current DACA holders, operating under injunctions that preserve the status quo. **Initial applications for first-time applicants remain blocked.**

Your current DACA approval is valid and legally enforceable today. But the program's long-term status is not settled, and courts could issue rulings at any time that alter what USCIS can process. This is why timely renewal and professional guidance matter.

## What To Do If You're a DACA Recipient in Georgia

Here are five concrete steps to protect your status right now:

1. **Know your expiration date.** Pull out your EAD card and confirm when it expires. Set a reminder to file 150–180 days in advance.
2. **Keep your address current with USCIS.** File Form AR-11 within 10 days of any move. Notices sent to an old address can cause you to miss critical deadlines.
3. **Don't travel internationally without advance parole.** Leaving the U.S. without USCIS approval typically abandons your DACA and can trigger bars to reentry. Speak to an attorney before boarding any international flight.
4. **Document your continuous presence.** Keep organized records — tax returns, school transcripts, employment records, utility bills — that prove continuous U.S. residence since 2007.
5. **Consult a DACA attorney in Georgia.** Even if your current situation feels stable, a consultation can identify vulnerabilities in your case and alternative immigration pathways you may not know exist.

If you've already missed a renewal window or experienced a lapse in coverage, don't wait. Contact an immigration attorney immediately to assess your options, including whether family-based or humanitarian relief may provide an alternative path forward.
