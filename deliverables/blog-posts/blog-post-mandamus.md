# How to Force USCIS to Act: When a Mandamus Lawsuit Is Your Best Option

You filed your application. You paid the fees. You showed up for biometrics. And then — nothing. Months turned into years. You checked the case status online so many times you have it memorized. You called USCIS and got the same scripted answer. Maybe your member of Congress even sent an inquiry, and still the case sits.

If this is your reality, understand one thing clearly: you are not powerless, and you do not have to keep waiting indefinitely. Federal law gives you a tool to make USCIS do its job — a mandamus lawsuit.

---

## The Backlog Crisis: You Shouldn't Have to Wait Years

USCIS is sitting on a staggering number of cases. As of 2026, hundreds of thousands of applications remain pending across the system — green card applications, naturalization cases, family petitions, and more. For many applicants, the wait is not months but years. Three years. Five years. In some cases, seven or more.

These delays are not just frustrating. They have real consequences: families kept apart across borders, careers put on hold, the inability to travel, and the constant uncertainty of living without a decision. You did everything the government asked. You shouldn't have to put your life on indefinite hold because an agency won't act.

The good news is that the law agrees with you.

---

## What Is a Mandamus Lawsuit?

A writ of mandamus is a lawsuit you file in federal district court asking a judge to order a government agency to take an action it is legally required to take. In the immigration context, that means suing USCIS (or, in some cases, the State Department or National Visa Center) to compel a decision on a case that has been stuck for an unreasonable amount of time.

Here is the key distinction: a mandamus lawsuit does not ask the court to *approve* your application. It asks the court to force USCIS to *decide* it — one way or the other. You are not suing because USCIS said no. You are suing because USCIS won't say anything at all.

For people who have waited years in silence, that distinction matters less than you might think. After years of limbo, getting an actual decision is often exactly what a client needs to move forward.

---

## When Does a Mandamus Lawsuit Make Sense?

The legal standard courts apply is **unreasonable delay**. There is no magic number that automatically qualifies, but several factors strengthen a case:

- **How long the case has been pending.** Delays of two or more years generally provide a strong foundation. Some cases pending less time qualify too, depending on the circumstances.
- **The type of application.** Routine adjudications that should move on a predictable timeline are stronger candidates than cases involving complex security review.
- **Prior inquiries to USCIS.** A documented history of case status inquiries, service requests, and follow-ups shows the court you have tried every normal channel.
- **A Congressional inquiry already attempted.** If your senator or representative submitted an inquiry and USCIS still didn't act, that strengthens the argument that litigation is the only remaining option.

If you have waited years, exhausted the normal channels, and there is no active reason for the delay — such as a genuine pending background check — a mandamus lawsuit is often the most effective tool available.

---

## What Types of Cases Qualify?

Many categories of unreasonably delayed cases can support a mandamus action, including:

- **N-400 naturalization** — citizenship applications stalled after the interview with no decision issued.
- **Adjustment of status (I-485)** — family-based and employment-based green card applications pending for years with no interview and no requests for evidence.
- **I-130 family petitions** — petitions filed by U.S. citizens and lawful permanent residents that sit unadjudicated long past normal processing times.
- **Asylum cases** — affirmative asylum applications subject to extraordinary delays at the asylum office.

The common thread is not the form number — it is the delay. If a federal agency has a clear legal duty to act on your case and has failed to do so within a reasonable time, you may have grounds to file.

---

## What to Expect

A mandamus case begins by filing a complaint in the U.S. District Court for the district where you live. In Georgia, that is typically the Northern District in Atlanta, with the Middle and Southern Districts covering other parts of the state. The government — USCIS, the U.S. Attorney's office, and related officials — is then formally served.

Here is what surprises many clients: the lawsuit itself often does the work. Once the government is served and the case lands on a U.S. Attorney's desk, the agency frequently adjudicates the long-stalled application rather than litigate. In many cases, USCIS acts well before the matter ever reaches a judge.

A realistic overall timeline runs roughly **6 to 18 months** from filing to resolution, though many cases conclude faster when the agency acts early. Your attorney handles the drafting, filing, and court communication. Your job is to provide the documentation: receipts, status inquiry responses, and a clear record of how long you have waited.

A mandamus lawsuit cannot guarantee any particular result on your underlying application, and no honest attorney would promise one. What it can do is break the silence and force a decision.

Many immigration attorneys have never set foot in federal court and do not handle these cases at all.
