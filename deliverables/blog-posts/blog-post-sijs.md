# SIJS Explained: Immigration Relief for Undocumented Children in the U.S.

*Si usted está ayudando a un niño o joven indocumentado, es posible que califique para SIJS — un camino hacia la residencia legal. Siga leyendo.*

For a child who came to the United States fleeing abuse, neglect, or abandonment, the immigration system can feel like another barrier — another institution that wasn't built with them in mind.

Special Immigrant Juvenile Status (SIJS) was built with them in mind. It is a federal immigration classification that lets certain undocumented children and young adults apply for a green card without the help — or the cooperation — of the parents who harmed or abandoned them. It recognizes a simple truth: some children cannot safely return to the country they came from, and some families cannot be reunified.

Here's what you need to know.

---

## What Is SIJS?

Special Immigrant Juvenile Status is an immigration classification created by Congress for undocumented minors who have been abused, neglected, or abandoned — and for whom it would not be in their best interest to return to their home country.

SIJS is not asylum. It does not require the child to prove they were persecuted on account of race, religion, or political opinion. Instead, it begins in state court, where a family or juvenile court judge makes findings about the child's situation — findings that then support a petition to U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services (USCIS). That classification can ultimately lead to lawful permanent residence — a green card — for a child who might otherwise spend years in legal limbo.

---

## Who Can Apply for SIJS?

To be eligible for Special Immigrant Juvenile Status, a child or young adult must:

- **Age:** Be under 21 when the petition is filed with USCIS
- **Marital status:** Be unmarried
- **Location:** Be present in the United States
- **State court order:** Have a valid order from a state juvenile or family court finding that:
  - The child has been abused, neglected, or abandoned by one or both parents
  - Reunification with one or both parents is not viable
  - It is not in the child's best interest to be returned to their home country

Importantly, SIJS does not require that *both* parents be unfit. A finding that reunification with just *one* parent is not viable — even if the other parent is safe — is enough to support a petition. The child also does not need to be in foster care; guardianship and probate proceedings can produce the required court order, too.

---

## How SIJS Leads to a Green Card

SIJS is not a green card in itself — it's a classification that opens the door to one. The process unfolds in stages:

**Step 1 — State court order.** A family or juvenile court issues findings about the child's situation. This is the foundation of the case; without a valid order, there is no SIJS petition.

**Step 2 — SIJS petition (Form I-360).** The attorney files this petition asking USCIS to classify the child as a Special Immigrant Juvenile based on the court's findings.

**Step 3 — Visa availability.** SIJS falls under the EB-4 category. For children born in Mexico, El Salvador, Guatemala, or Honduras, there may be a wait before a visa number becomes available; children from other countries usually have immediate or near-immediate availability.

**Step 4 — Adjustment of status (Form I-485).** Once a visa number is available, the child applies for lawful permanent residence. If approved, they receive a green card.

The pathway is real, but it takes careful coordination between the state court case and the federal immigration process — each with its own timeline and paperwork.

---

## The Role of the Juvenile Court

SIJS is unusual because it begins in state court, not at USCIS. A federal green card ultimately depends on findings made by a state judge — and those findings have to be specific. The order must address:

- The child's dependency on the court, or placement with a guardian under state law
- Whether reunification with one or both parents is viable, and why
- Whether returning to the child's home country is in their best interest

Not every family court judge is familiar with the exact language USCIS requires. This is one of the most common places cases stall: the order is issued, but it lacks the right findings, and USCIS denies the I-360 as a result.

An immigration attorney who handles SIJS works *alongside* the family law case — often coordinating with the child's DFCS caseworker, guardian ad litem (GAL), or family court attorney to make sure the order captures everything USCIS needs. It is a collaboration between two legal systems, and both sides have to get it right.

---

## Why Acting Quickly Matters

Time is critical in SIJS cases for two reasons.

**Aging out.** Eligibility requires the child to be under 21 when the I-360 is filed. Once they turn 21, the window closes permanently. The state court process, attorney preparation, and USCIS processing all take time — waiting too long, even by a few months, can mean losing eligibility entirely.

**Visa backlogs.** For children from certain countries, particularly in Central America and Mexico, the EB-4 backlog can add years. The earlier the I-360 is filed and approved, the earlier the child's priority date is set — which determines their place in line for a visa number.

If a child in your care may qualify, early consultation is not optional. It is how you protect the opportunity.
