# SIJS Explained: Immigration Relief for Undocumented Children in the U.S.

*Si usted está ayudando a un niño o joven indocumentado, es posible que califique para SIJS — un camino hacia la residencia legal. Siga leyendo.*

For a child who came to the United States fleeing abuse, neglect, or abandonment, the immigration system can feel like another barrier — another institution that wasn't built with them in mind.

Special Immigrant Juvenile Status (SIJS) was built with them in mind.

SIJS is a federal immigration classification that allows certain undocumented children and young adults to apply for a green card without the help — or the cooperation — of the parents who harmed or abandoned them. It recognizes a simple truth: some children cannot safely return to the country they came from, and some families cannot be reunified. Immigration law has a specific answer for that situation.

Here's what you need to know.

---

## What Is SIJS?

Special Immigrant Juvenile Status is an immigration classification created by Congress specifically for undocumented minors who have been abused, neglected, or abandoned — and for whom it would not be in their best interest to return to their home country.

SIJS is not the same as asylum. It does not require the child to prove they were persecuted on account of race, religion, or political opinion. Instead, it starts in state court, where a family or juvenile court judge makes findings about the child's situation — findings that then support a petition to U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services (USCIS).

A SIJS classification can ultimately lead to lawful permanent residence — a green card — which is a life-changing outcome for a child who might otherwise spend years in legal limbo.

---

## Who Can Apply for SIJS?

To be eligible for Special Immigrant Juvenile Status, a child or young adult must meet several requirements:

- **Age:** Under 21 years old at the time the petition is filed with USCIS
- **Marital status:** Unmarried
- **Location:** Present in the United States
- **State court involvement:** Must have a valid order from a state juvenile or family court
- **Court findings:** The state court must have found that:
  - The child has been abused, neglected, or abandoned by one or both parents
  - Reunification with one or both parents is not viable
  - It is not in the child's best interest to be returned to their home country

Importantly, SIJS does not require that *both* parents be unfit. A court finding that reunification with just *one* parent is not viable — while the other parent may be safe — is sufficient to support a SIJS petition.

The child does not need to be in foster care or involved in a formal dependency case, though many SIJS applicants are in the child welfare system. Guardianship and probate proceedings can also produce the court order needed.

---

## How SIJS Leads to a Green Card

SIJS is not a green card in itself — it's a classification that opens the door to one. The process unfolds in stages:

**Step 1 — State court order.** A family or juvenile court issues findings about the child's situation. This is the foundation of the entire case. Without a valid court order, there is no SIJS petition.

**Step 2 — SIJS petition (Form I-360).** The immigration attorney files a Petition for Amerasian, Widow(er), or Special Immigrant with USCIS. This petition asks USCIS to classify the child as a Special Immigrant Juvenile based on the state court findings.

**Step 3 — Visa availability.** SIJS falls under the EB-4 immigrant visa category. For children born in Mexico, El Salvador, Guatemala, or Honduras — countries with historically high demand — there may be a waiting period before a visa number becomes available. Children born in other countries typically have immediate or near-immediate visa availability.

**Step 4 — Adjustment of status (Form I-485).** Once a visa number is available, the child applies for lawful permanent residence. If approved, they receive a green card.

The pathway is real, but it takes careful coordination between the state court proceeding and the federal immigration process. Each stage has its own timeline, requirements, and paperwork.

---

## The Role of the Juvenile Court

SIJS is unusual in immigration law because it begins in state court, not at USCIS. A federal green card ultimately depends on findings made by a state judge — and those findings have to be specific.

The court order must include language addressing:

- The child's dependency on the court, or placement with a guardian under state law
- Whether reunification with one or both parents is viable, and why or why not
- Whether return to the child's home country is in their best interest

Not every family court judge is familiar with SIJS or the specific language USCIS requires. This is one of the most common places where cases stall or fail: the state court order is issued, but it doesn't contain the right findings, and USCIS denies the I-360 as a result.

An immigration attorney who handles SIJS cases works *alongside* the family law proceeding — often coordinating with the child's DFCS caseworker, guardian ad litem (GAL), or family court attorney to ensure the court order captures everything USCIS needs. It is a collaboration between two separate legal systems, and both sides have to work together to get it right.

---

## Why Acting Quickly Matters

Time is a critical factor in SIJS cases for two reasons.

**Aging out.** Eligibility requires that the child be under 21 at the time the I-360 is filed with USCIS. Once a child turns 21, the window closes permanently. The state court process takes time. Attorney preparation takes time. Filing and USCIS processing take time. Waiting too long — even by a few months — can mean losing eligibility entirely.

**Visa backlogs.** For children from certain countries, particularly those in Central America and Mexico, the EB-4 visa backlog can add years to the process. The earlier the I-360 is filed and approved, the earlier the child's priority date is established — which determines their place in line for a visa number.

If a child in your care may qualify for SIJS, early consultation with an immigration attorney is not optional. It is how you protect the opportunity.
