## What Is Temporary Protected Status?

TPS was created by Congress in the Immigration Act of 1990 and is set out in Section 244 of the Immigration and Nationality Act. It allows the Secretary of Homeland Security to designate a foreign country for temporary protection when conditions there make it unsafe for nationals to return. A country can be designated for one of three reasons:

- **Ongoing armed conflict**, such as a civil war, that would pose a serious threat to personal safety.
- **An environmental disaster or epidemic**, such as an earthquake, hurricane, or public-health crisis, that disrupts living conditions.
- **Other extraordinary and temporary conditions** that prevent nationals from returning safely.

A designation is granted for a fixed period, typically 6, 12, or 18 months, and the Secretary must decide before each period ends whether to extend, redesignate, or terminate it. That review cycle is why TPS is never permanent and why deadlines matter so much.

## What TPS Provides in 2026

While your country's designation is in effect and your status is valid, TPS gives you three concrete protections:

- **Protection from removal.** You cannot be deported from the United States based solely on your immigration status during the designation period.
- **Work authorization.** TPS makes you eligible for an Employment Authorization Document (EAD) so you can work legally.
- **Possible travel authorization.** You may apply for permission to travel abroad and return, but you should never leave the country without that authorization in hand, and never without speaking to an attorney first.

## Who Is Eligible for TPS

Eligibility is country-specific and date-specific, but the core requirements are consistent. To qualify, you generally must:

- Be a **national of a designated country** (or a person without nationality who last habitually resided there).
- Have been **continuously physically present** in the United States since the effective date of your country's most recent designation.
- Have **continuously resided** in the United States since the specific date set for your country.
- File **during the open registration or re-registration window**; late filings are accepted only in limited circumstances.

Certain criminal and security issues can bar you from TPS. In general, a conviction for any felony, or for two or more misdemeanors committed in the United States, makes you ineligible, and other grounds of inadmissibility can apply. If you have any arrest or conviction in your history, talk to a TPS attorney before you file, because what looks like a minor matter can quietly disqualify you.

## TPS in 2026: A Program in Flux

Here is the part that makes 2026 different from past years. Over the last year, several high-profile TPS designations have been the subject of agency decisions to terminate or shorten them, followed by lawsuits challenging those decisions in federal court. The result is a moving target: a country's status can be announced as ending, then paused by a court, then changed again on appeal.

For that reason, this article will not list specific expiration dates by country, because the accurate answer can change between the time this is written and the time you read it. Instead, follow two rules:

- **Check the official source.** The USCIS TPS page publishes the current designation status, registration windows, and any automatic EAD extensions for each country. That page, not social media or word of mouth, is the authority.
- **Get advice the moment you hear your country's status may change.** If a termination is announced, there is often a window to act, re-register, apply for a different benefit, or join a legal challenge, and that window can be short.

## What TPS Does Not Do

This is one of the most common and costly misunderstandings. TPS is protection, not a path. By itself, it does *not* give you a green card or U.S. citizenship, and time spent in TPS does not automatically convert into permanent residence. Some TPS holders may be able to pursue a green card through a separate avenue, for example a family petition, but whether that works depends on how you entered the country and other technical factors that have been litigated all the way to the Supreme Court. This is exactly the kind of question to bring to an attorney rather than guess at.

## Registration and Re-Registration: Don't Miss Your Window

TPS is not something you file once and forget. To keep your status and your work permit valid, you must **re-register during every open window** that USCIS announces for your country. Key points to keep in mind in 2026:

- **Re-registration is time-limited.** Each window has a firm start and end date. Filing late, without an accepted reason, can cost you your status.
- **Your EAD may or may not be auto-extended.** When a designation is extended, USCIS sometimes automatically extends existing work permits for a set period. Whether that applies to you depends on the specific notice, confirm it before assuming you can keep working.
- **Keep proof of your continuous presence.** Pay stubs, leases, tax returns, and medical or school records help establish that you have met the physical-presence and residence requirements if USCIS questions them.

## What Happens If Your Country's TPS Ends

If a designation is terminated and survives any court challenge, protection and work authorization end on the date the government sets. That does not always mean you are out of options, but it does mean the options become time-sensitive. Depending on your situation, you may be able to:

- Apply for another immigration benefit you separately qualify for, such as a green card through a U.S. citizen spouse or relative (family-based immigration).
- Seek protection such as asylum, withholding of removal, or relief tied to being a crime victim, if the facts of your case support it.
- Prepare a defense if you are placed in removal proceedings, rather than facing immigration court unrepresented.

The worst outcome is doing nothing until status has already lapsed. If you think your country's designation is at risk, that is the moment to plan, not after.
