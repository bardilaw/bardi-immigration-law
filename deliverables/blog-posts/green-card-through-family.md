# Green Card Through Family: A Complete Guide for Immigrants in Georgia

If you are trying to bring a husband, wife, child, or parent to live with you in the United States — or you want to help a loved one already here get their green card — the process can feel overwhelming. The forms are long. The waits are long. And one small mistake can cost you years. A family green card attorney in Georgia can help you avoid those mistakes and keep your family together.

You do not have to figure it out alone. The right attorney guides your family through each step, protects you from the errors that delay or derail cases, and helps you keep everyone together. This guide explains how family-based green cards work, who can petition for whom, the two paths to permanent residence, and what to watch out for along the way.

---

## Why Family Comes First in U.S. Immigration

Family reunification is the heart of the U.S. immigration system. More people get green cards through family than through any other path — more than through jobs, more than through the visa lottery. Congress built it this way on purpose. The law recognizes that families belong together, and it gives U.S. citizens and lawful permanent residents the right to petition for certain relatives to join them.

For families across Georgia and the Southeast, that promise is real — but it comes with rules, paperwork, and waiting. Understanding how the system works is the first step to using it well.

---

## Who Can Petition for a Family Member?

Not everyone can petition, and not every relative qualifies. Two things decide what is possible: **who you are** and **how you are related.**

### Citizens vs. lawful permanent residents

- **U.S. citizens** can petition for the widest circle of relatives: spouses, children (of any age, married or unmarried), parents, and brothers and sisters.
- **Lawful permanent residents (green card holders)** can petition for a smaller group: spouses and unmarried children only. They cannot petition for parents or siblings.

### Immediate relatives vs. preference categories

This is the most important distinction in the whole process, because it decides how long your family will wait.

**Immediate relatives** of U.S. citizens have *no annual limit* on green cards. That means no waiting line for a visa number. This group includes:

- Spouses of U.S. citizens
- Unmarried children under 21 of U.S. citizens
- Parents of U.S. citizens (the citizen must be 21 or older)

**Preference categories** *do* have annual limits, so a line forms — sometimes a very long one. These include adult children, married children, siblings of citizens, and the spouses and children of green card holders. Depending on the category and the country, the wait can run from a couple of years to well over a decade.

Figuring out which bucket your relative falls into is where many families need a **family-based green card** attorney in Georgia. Get the category right, and you can plan realistically. Get it wrong, and you may wait for a line you were never actually in.

## The Two Paths: Adjustment of Status vs. Consular Processing

There are two ways to actually get the green card. Which one applies depends mostly on where your relative is and how they entered the country.

### Adjustment of status (the relative is already in the U.S.)

**Adjustment of status** lets someone already inside the United States apply for a green card without leaving. They file with U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services (USCIS), attend a local interview, and — if approved — get their green card by mail. This is usually the path when a relative entered legally and is eligible to adjust.

For families, the big advantage is staying together. Nobody has to leave the country and risk being stuck abroad.

### Consular processing (the relative is outside the U.S.)

**Consular processing** is used when the relative lives abroad, or cannot adjust from inside the U.S. The case moves from USCIS to the National Visa Center and then to a U.S. embassy or consulate in the relative's home country, where they attend an interview. If approved, they receive an immigrant visa, enter the U.S., and become a permanent resident.

Choosing the wrong path — or worse, assuming someone can adjust when they cannot — is one of the most damaging mistakes in family immigration. Leaving the country at the wrong moment can trigger a bar that keeps a spouse locked out for **three or ten years.** This is exactly the kind of risk an attorney is there to catch *before* it happens.

## The I-130 Explained: Where Every Family Case Begins

Almost every family green card starts with one form: the **I-130, Petition for Alien Relative.**

### What the I-130 actually does

The **I-130 petition** does one job: it proves the family relationship is real and qualifies under the law. A citizen or green card holder (the "petitioner") files it for their relative (the "beneficiary"). Approval of the I-130 does *not* grant a green card. It simply establishes that a valid relationship exists and reserves the relative's place in line if a line applies.

### What to expect

You will need documents that prove the relationship — a marriage certificate, birth certificates, photos, proof of a shared life for married couples, and more. USCIS reviews everything and may ask for additional evidence. For marriage cases especially, the agency looks closely to make sure the marriage is genuine and not just for immigration.

### How long it takes

Timelines vary a lot. For immediate relatives, the I-130 and green card application can sometimes be filed together and finished in roughly a year to a year and a half. For preference categories, the I-130 may be approved fairly quickly, but the family then waits — sometimes years — for a visa number to become available. Filing an **I-130 petition** in Georgia follows the same federal rules as anywhere else, but local processing times and interview backlogs at offices like the Atlanta field office affect how fast your case moves.

## Common Mistakes That Delay or Derail Family Green Card Cases

Most family cases that fall apart do so for avoidable reasons. The most common:

- **Filing in the wrong category** — claiming immediate-relative status when the relative is actually in a preference line, or the reverse.
- **Weak proof of a real marriage** — thin documentation invites suspicion, a longer interview, or a fraud finding.
- **Missing the income requirement** — the petitioner must file an Affidavit of Support showing enough income. Fall short without a joint sponsor, and the case stalls.
- **Traveling at the wrong time** — leaving the U.S. with a pending case, or before the right step, can trigger the three- and ten-year bars.
- **Ignoring prior immigration or criminal history** — past entries, overstays, removals, or arrests can change everything if they are not handled correctly from the start.
- **Mistakes on the forms** — wrong dates, missing signatures, and inconsistent answers lead to requests for evidence that add months.

Each of these is fixable — *before* you file. After you file, some become very hard to undo.

## Why an Immigration Attorney Matters

Plenty of straightforward cases get approved without a lawyer. But "straightforward" is doing a lot of work in that sentence — and many families do not know their case is complicated until it is too late.

You especially need an experienced attorney if there are any of these in the picture:

- **A prior entry without inspection** (crossing without being admitted)
- **An overstay** of a prior visa
- **A prior removal or deportation order**
- **Any criminal history**, even old or minor charges
- **A previous denial** or a prior marriage-based case

In these situations, the wrong move can do more than delay a case — it can get a loved one detained or barred from the country for years. A skilled **family green card attorney** in Georgia spots these risks early, chooses the safe path, and prepares the evidence so your family's case is as strong as it can be the first time.
