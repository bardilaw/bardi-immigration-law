"What's the difference between a green card and citizenship?"

This is one of the most common questions we hear — and it's an important one.

🟦 Green Card (Lawful Permanent Resident status):
• You can live and work in the U.S. permanently
• You must renew your green card every 10 years
• You can be removed from the U.S. under certain circumstances (criminal convictions, abandonment)
• You generally cannot vote in federal elections
• Travel outside the U.S. for long periods can affect your residence

🟨 U.S. Citizenship (Naturalization):
• You have full constitutional rights, including the right to vote
• You can get a U.S. passport
• Your status cannot be revoked by removal proceedings
• You can petition for a wider range of family members
• No renewal required

Most people who are eligible for naturalization benefit from pursuing it. The requirements include 5 years as a permanent resident (or 3 years if married to a U.S. citizen), continuous residence, good moral character, and passing the civics test.

A question I receive more often than expected, even from clients who've been permanent residents for years:

"What's actually the difference between a green card and citizenship — and do I need to become a citizen?"

The short answer: you don't have to. But for most clients, there are meaningful legal and practical reasons to pursue naturalization once eligible.

For practitioners who work with immigrant clients:

→ LPR status is revocable. Criminal convictions, extended absences, and abandonment findings can all result in removal proceedings — even for long-time residents. Citizenship eliminates that exposure.
→ Naturalization unlocks immediate relative petitions. A citizen can petition for a spouse, child, or parent without waiting on a visa bulletin.
→ Dual intent matters. Some clients hesitate because they believe naturalization means surrendering their home country citizenship. That's a country-by-country question, not a U.S. law question.

If your clients are approaching their 5-year eligibility window and haven't asked the naturalization question, it may be worth raising. I'm happy to be a resource. bardilaw.com
