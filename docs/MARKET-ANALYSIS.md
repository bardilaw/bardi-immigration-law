## 1. Target Market

### Service Mix (per Client Brief)

Bardi Immigration Law is a solo, boutique practice with three concentric service lines:

1. **Benefits-based immigration** — naturalization, LPR / green cards, family-based petitions (I-130), TPS, waivers (I-601, I-212, I-929), DACA, SIJS, VAWA, U-visa.
2. **Removal defense** — detained and non-detained removal, bond hearings, cancellation of removal (42B non-LPR and 42A LPR).
3. **Federal litigation** — habeas corpus and mandamus petitions.

Personal injury is flagged for the future; out of scope for the June 10, 2026 launch.

### Primary Segments

| Segment | Description | Primary Service Match |
|---------|-------------|----------------------|
| **Long-resident young adults** (Millennial / Gen-Z) | Brought to the US as children; normalizing status via marriage, humanitarian relief, or revival of a parent's prior I-130 | DACA, AOS, I-130, U-visa, VAWA, waivers |
| **Unaccompanied minors (under 18)** | In the US without a parent; eligible for state-court findings + federal humanitarian relief | SIJS, full lifecycle representation |
| **Removal defense clients** | Adults of any age facing NTAs, detention, or active proceedings; often long US residency | 42B/42A cancellation, bond, detained docket, habeas / mandamus |
| **Mixed-status families** | US citizen / LPR petitioning a spouse, child, or parent — with or without unlawful presence complications | I-130, AOS, consular processing, I-601 / I-212 waivers |
| **TPS-eligible nationals** | Beneficiaries from designated countries (e.g., Venezuela, Honduras, El Salvador, Haiti) needing initial or re-registration filings | TPS, work authorization, adjustment when paired with petition |

### Core Problem

The clients Bardi serves face high-stakes, opaque, and constantly-shifting legal terrain. Without competent representation they risk deportation, loss of work authorization, inability to get a driver's license, and family separation. The market they navigate is dominated by:

- **Notarios and unauthorized practitioners** preying on Spanish-dominant clients
- **High-volume "immigration mills"** that route clients through intake specialists before any attorney touches the file
- **Generalist law firms** that take immigration as a side practice without specialized competence
- **Premium firms** priced beyond the $30K–$80K household-income middle of Bardi's ICP

The unmet need is **a credentialed, accessible attorney who personally manages the case** — at a price point and tempo that working immigrant families can sustain.

## 2. Geographic Focus

**Primary:** Georgia (largest client share)
**Secondary:** North Carolina, South Carolina, Alabama
**National reach:** Federal litigation (habeas, mandamus) and complex matters can be taken anywhere in the US; website should signal national capacity while messaging the four-state core.

Within Georgia, Gwinnett County (~26% foreign-born) and the broader Atlanta MSA remain dense lead pools, but the practice is **regional, not local**. Website architecture, schema, and ad targeting should reflect a four-state footprint, not Atlanta-only.

Immigrant communities of note in the footprint:
- **Hispanic/Latino** (Mexican, Guatemalan, Salvadoran, Honduran, Venezuelan) — primary Spanish-speaking audience; rationale for full Spanish translation of the site
- **Haitian Creole, Brazilian Portuguese, Asian-language communities** — present but secondary; not a Milestone 1 translation requirement

## 3. Demand Signals

- **Search volume (Google Keyword Planner estimates, moderate confidence):**
  - "immigration lawyer Atlanta" — ~2,400–3,600/mo
  - "immigration attorney near me" Atlanta MSA — ~1,800–2,400/mo
  - "DACA renewal lawyer" + state modifiers (GA/NC/SC) — ~600–1,200/mo combined
  - "abogado de inmigración" + GA/NC — ~1,500–2,500/mo combined
  - "deportation lawyer" / "removal defense attorney" Atlanta / Charlotte — ~800–1,400/mo combined
- **DACA pipeline:** ~52K active DACA recipients across the 4 states. Renewals are biennial and policy volatility (litigation, USCIS guidance changes) creates recurring demand.
- **Removal defense:** Atlanta and Charlotte immigration courts are among the most backlogged in the country. Stewart Detention Center generates a continuous detained docket needing bond and merits representation.
- **Family-based petitions:** I-130 remains the largest filing category nationally; mixed-status families in the footprint are a steady, referral-driven base.
- **Humanitarian relief:** U-visa wait times (years-long backlog) and VAWA filings continue to generate demand from survivors of crime and domestic violence. SIJS volume tracks unaccompanied minor arrivals.
- **Federal litigation:** Mandamus filings to compel USCIS action on unreasonably-delayed cases are a growing niche — few Southeast firms litigate them; Bardi's federal-court experience is a differentiator.

## 8. Recommendations (Pre-Launch, June 10, 2026)

These recommendations are scoped to the three-week launch window. Anything not addressable by June 10 moves to a Milestone 2 backlog.

1. **Build the launch site around three pillars, not five.** Benefits, Removal Defense, and Federal Litigation get top-nav. DACA, SIJS, VAWA, U-visa, TPS, and waivers are sub-pages under Benefits. This matches how clients describe their problem ("I'm in deportation," "I want a green card") instead of how attorneys file forms.

2. **Ship full Spanish translation at launch (not Phase 2).** Per CLIENT-BRIEF §7, this is a baseline requirement. Use parallel URL structure (`/es/...`) with hreflang tags. Translation must be human or human-reviewed — auto-translation is a credibility risk for an attorney-quality brand.

3. **State-specific landing pages for GA, NC, SC, AL.** One page per state per primary service (benefits, removal). Include court venue, attorney bar admissions, and state-specific procedural notes. Captures regional intent and signals four-state capacity.

4. **DACA, U-visa, VAWA, and SIJS content cluster as the SEO anchor.** Higher intent and lower competition than generic "immigration lawyer" queries. Three pillar pages + 3–5 supporting articles each is achievable in the launch window.

5. **Lead the homepage with the "direct attorney" promise.** Above-the-fold copy should make this concrete: "Your first call is with the attorney, not an intake specialist." This is the single sharpest differentiator from Diaz & Gaeta, mills, and notarios.

6. **Set up Google Business Profile and a 60-day review playbook before launch.** Local SEO is the #1 acquisition channel; aim for 10+ Google reviews within 60 days post-launch. Solicit from recent clients, AILA colleagues, and referral attorneys.

7. **Publish flat fees on three services at launch: DACA renewal, naturalization, and uncomplicated AOS.** Trust signal and notario-comparison weapon. Complex matters remain quoted individually.

8. **Defer (Milestone 2):**
   - Personal injury practice area (per CLIENT-BRIEF §2 — flagged but out of scope)
   - Direct online booking (per CLIENT-BRIEF §9 — client undecided; ship with contact form + phone for launch, decide on Calendly-style flow in M2)
   - Additional language translations beyond Spanish
   - Paid Google / Meta campaigns (launch with organic + GBP; layer paid in M2 once content and conversion data exist)
